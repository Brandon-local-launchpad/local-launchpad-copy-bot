## WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE — CATEGORY BLOCKS

The following are real examples written for a landscaping homepage. They are included so you understand the standard required.

Note: these examples use actual words rather than placeholders so the standard is easy to read. In your output, every business name, area, and category must use the correct GHL placeholder.

### Garden Maintenance

Southampton gardens range from compact terraced plots in Shirley and Bevois Valley to larger family gardens in Bassett and Highfield. Regular garden maintenance here means dealing with clay heavy ground, persistent weed pressure from bindweed and ground elder, and dense overgrowth that builds quickly in Hampshire's mild, wet climate. See everything that is covered in our garden maintenance service in Southampton.

**Why this works:**
- Names specific Southampton neighbourhoods (Shirley, Bevois Valley, Bassett, Highfield) with a reason — they illustrate the range of property types, not just local colour
- Names actual weed species (bindweed, ground elder) — real horticultural knowledge that generic AI copy would not include
- Connects Hampshire's mild, wet climate to a specific consequence (fast overgrowth) rather than just mentioning the weather
- The editorial link names the category and the location — Google knows exactly what the destination page covers

### Lawn Care

Southampton lawns take a beating through winter. Waterlogging in low lying gardens, compaction from heavy use, and persistent couch grass and annual meadow grass pressure all contribute to lawns that need more than a regular cut. Aeration, top dressing, overseeding, and weed treatment are often what actually fixes the problem. Find out everything included in our lawn care service in Southampton.

**Why this works:**
- Opens with a specific consequence (winter damage) not a vague claim
- Names actual services within the category (aeration, top dressing, overseeding, weed treatment) — tells Google what the category page covers before the visitor even clicks
- Names actual weed species specific to Hampshire (couch grass, annual meadow grass)
- "More than a regular cut" positions the category correctly without using banned phrases
- Editorial link is descriptive and contains both the category name and the location

### Fencing

Fencing in Southampton's more exposed areas takes more punishment than most homeowners expect. Wind stress near the Solent edge, saturated ground through winter, and ageing posts in older properties all accelerate deterioration. From garden fencing to agricultural stock fencing and timber gates, proper installation makes the difference. See what our fencing service includes for Southampton properties.

**Why this works:**
- Opens with a locally specific cause (Solent wind exposure) not a generic statement about fencing
- Three distinct deterioration causes named (wind stress, saturated ground, ageing posts in older properties) — each one traceable to the geo research
- Names actual product types within the category (garden fencing, agricultural stock fencing, timber gates)
- Editorial link connects back to Southampton properties specifically

### Patios and Driveways

Patios and driveways in Southampton need to be laid on properly prepared ground, especially where clay soil or poor drainage sits beneath the surface. Block paving, brick patios, and gravel driveways all behave differently on unstable or wet subgrades. Shortcuts at the base stage are what cause problems two winters later. Find out how our paving service approaches groundwork and installation in Southampton.

**Why this works:**
- Leads with the local soil and drainage problem before naming the service types
- The "two winters later" line is specific and credible — it tells the visitor exactly what poor installation looks like in real terms
- Names actual product types (block paving, brick patios, gravel driveways)
- Editorial link references both the process (groundwork and installation) and the location — more informative than a generic link

## WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE — FAQ ANSWERS

These show how to apply the editorial link standard inside FAQ answers, and how to handle a missing fact correctly when it occurs.

### Example 1 — FAQ with editorial link

**Q: What kind of garden work do you take on in Southampton?**

A: The team focuses on larger, more involved projects rather than quick one off jobs — full garden makeovers, planting design, and ongoing maintenance for established gardens. Lawn care is one of the most requested services, particularly aeration and overseeding on Southampton's clay heavy lawns. Find out everything included in our lawn care service in Southampton to see what a typical programme covers.

**Why this works:**
- Answers the question with real scope detail (project size, named services) before the link
- Names a local condition (clay heavy lawns) tying the service to a genuine local need
- Editorial link names the category and the location and describes what the destination page covers
- Links to a category page, not back to the homepage

### Example 2 — FAQ with a missing fact handled correctly

**Q: Are you insured?**

A: Yes, the team carries public liability insurance covering all garden and landscaping work undertaken across Hampshire. [CLIENT TO CONFIRM: exact cover amount, e.g. £2m or £5m, to state the specific figure here.]

**Why this works:**
- The confirmed fact (insurance exists, covers all work, covers Hampshire) is stated plainly and confidently — the visitor is not left with an empty or evasive answer
- Only the genuinely unconfirmed detail (the specific cover amount) is flagged, and it is flagged as an addition to a real answer, not as the entire answer
- This is easy for a VA to spot and chase, and would never read as broken or unfinished if it accidentally made it to a live page, because the sentence around it still stands on its own as a true, complete claim

## WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE — MEET THE TEAM

### Example

**Headline:** Qualified, Experienced and Trusted Across Hampshire

**Body:**

Edens Edge landscaping was built on a straightforward idea, that great outdoor spaces deserve a team with real qualifications and real world experience behind them. Ethan and the team hold Level 3 qualifications across horticulture and construction, including Level 3 Extended Horticulture and Level 3 Carpentry, and that expertise shows in everything from a well laid patio to a fully managed Hampshire estate.

Ethan's background includes over 15 years working on private estates, alongside formal placements at RHS Wisley and Sir Harold Hillier Gardens, two of the most respected horticultural environments in the UK. Whether it's a full garden makeover for a large Hampshire property or specialist equestrian arena construction, Edens Edge landscaping brings the same confidence, craftsmanship, and care to every single job.

**Why this works:**
- The headline leads with three concrete trust attributes (qualified, experienced, trusted in a specific region) rather than labelling the section
- Named qualifications (Level 3 Extended Horticulture, Level 3 Carpentry) and named places (RHS Wisley, Sir Harold Hillier Gardens) are real, checkable facts — exactly the kind of detail that separates this from a generic about page
- "15 years working on private estates" and "specialist equestrian arena construction" are specific enough to be credible without sounding like a brochure
- No "we pride ourselves" or "we are passionate"

**One thing to flag:** the live version of this headline on Edens Edge's actual site reads "Qualified, Experienced & Genuinely Passionate About the Work." That's close to the standard above and the structure is good — three attributes, benefit led — but "Passionate" sits right next to the banned phrase "we are passionate" in spirit, even phrased in third person. When writing new headlines in this style, replace a feelings based word like "Passionate" with another fact based attribute (a place, a qualification, a number of years, a region covered) the way "Trusted Across Hampshire" does above.
